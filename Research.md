Foundational Theory of Market Structure and Trend DynamicsThe primary objective of technical analysis is to identify the underlying structure of a market, which is defined by the relationship between successive peaks and troughs, commonly referred to as swing highs and swing lows. A swing high is validated when a price peak is flanked by at least two lower highs on both sides, while a swing low is established when a trough is surrounded by at least two higher lows. These points are not merely historical markers; they represent critical shifts in the equilibrium between supply and demand. For a structural shift to be considered valid, price must break the previous swing high or low with a decisive candle body close. This distinction is critical for professional analysts, as a mere wick penetration without a body close is often categorized as a liquidity sweep rather than a structural reversal.Liquidity sweeps are essential mechanisms in modern markets, often occurring when price briefly pierces a prior swing point to trigger clustered stop-loss orders. This creates a pool of sell-side liquidity below swing lows and buy-side liquidity above swing highs, which institutional participants utilize to execute large orders with minimal slippage. Understanding these dynamics allows the analyst to distinguish between a genuine breakout and a trap designed to ensnare retail traders. A structural break, confirmed by a body close, signals that the market has accepted a new price range and is likely to continue in that direction.Trend identification follows a binary logic within this structural framework. An uptrend is confirmed by a sequence of higher highs and higher lows, indicating that buyers are consistently aggressive and willing to support price at increasingly elevated levels. Conversely, a downtrend is characterized by lower highs and lower lows, suggesting a dominant selling pressure. When peaks and troughs are flat or mixed, the market is described as being in a sideways range or attempting a reversal. Professional screening workflows require the analyst to connect the last three to four swing points to determine the current market bias. Geometry of Horizontal Support and Resistance ZonesSupport and resistance (S&R) levels function as the floor and ceiling of price movement, representing horizontal price bands where the market has historically demonstrated significant reactions. Support zones are areas where demand consistently absorbs selling pressure, often identified by multiple candle wick rejections or body clusters at a specific price range. Resistance zones represent the inverse, where supply overcomes demand, leading to price rejection. The reliability of these zones is proportional to the number of visible reactions; a level that has held twice is considered active, while a level with three or more touches is categorized as a major structural barrier.The Polarity Principle is a fundamental concept in S&R theory, stating that once a resistance level is breached by a decisive move, it often flips to become a support level upon a retest. This transition occurs due to the psychological behavior of market participants: traders who missed the initial breakout wait for a pullback to enter, and those who were short at resistance are forced to cover their positions at breakeven when price returns to that level, creating a surge in buying interest. Morphology of Candlestick Sentiment and Reversal SignalsCandlestick patterns provide a microscopic view of market sentiment within a single trading session. For a swing trade analyzer, the "Last Candle Reading" is the final technical filter that determines if an entry scenario is valid. These signals must be read precisely from the chart without invention. A Bullish Marubozu, characterized by a large bullish body with no wicks, signals strong buying conviction throughout the entire session. Conversely, a Bearish Marubozu indicates dominant selling pressure.More nuanced patterns, such as the Hammer and Shooting Star, represent price rejection. A Hammer forms when price moves significantly lower after the open but rallies to close near the top, leaving a long lower wick at least twice the length of the body. When this occurs at a proven support zone, it suggests that buyers have aggressively rejected lower prices, signaling that support appears to be holding. The Shooting Star is the bearish equivalent at resistance, where sellers reject higher prices, creating a long upper wick.Candlestick PatternVisual IdentificationMarket SentimentContextual SignificanceHammerSmall body, long lower wickBullish RejectionSupport holding indicatorShooting StarSmall body, long upper wickBearish RejectionResistance rejection indicatorBullish EngulfingLarge green covers prior redStrong ReversalDemand overcoming supplyDojiOpen and Close are equalMarket IndecisionEquilibrium between sidesInside BarContained in prior rangeVolatility CompressionPrecedes breakout movePin BarLong wick, tiny bodyExtreme RejectionRejection of a price levelThe presence of a Doji or a Spinning Top indicates market indecision, where neither buyers nor sellers are in control. Quantitative Risk Management and Position SizingThe transition from technical analysis to active trading is governed by the principles of quantitative risk management. The "1% Risk Rule" is the industry standard for capital preservation, suggesting that a trader should risk no more than 1% of their total trading capital on any single trade.The Algorithm for Calculated ExposurePosition sizing is a mathematical derivative of the distance between the entry price and the structural stop-loss level. A structural stop-loss is always anchored to the market's internal logic—typically placed just below the most recent swing low for a long position.$$Risk \ Capital = Total \ Capital \times 0.01$$$$Risk \ per \ Share = Entry \ Price - Stop \ Loss \ Price$$$$Position \ Size = \lfloor \frac{Risk \ Capital}{Risk \ per \ Share} \rfloor$$Risk-reward (RR) ratios further refine the selection. A setup is considered acceptable if the potential reward to the first visible resistance (Target 1) is at least 1.5 to 2 times the initial risk.VI.
